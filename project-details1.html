OK NOT OK

Mind your medication

Brief

An app measuring the relationship between psychiatric medication use and mood

Goal

To empower users to track and understand the impact of their psychiatric medication

Roles

UX Researcher
UX Designer

Tools

Figma
Visual Studio Code

THE PROBLEM

The impact of psychiatric medication on the human brain is inadequately measured and narrowly understood

The system used to diagnose and treat mental health conditions is rooted in observational findings that have yet to be biologically validated despite over 60 years of research.

As a result, professionals prescribe medications based on trial and error, without individualized data on how they uniquely interact with patients.

This approach leaves patients struggling to find the right treatment and prevents a broader understanding of how these medications affect overall mood and well-being.

DESIGNING A SOLUTION

Talking to psychiatric medication users

I interviewed five people who had been taking psychiatric medication daily for at least 6 months.

The goal was to understand their daily experiences, focusing on mood, symptoms, and medication-related challenges.

Considering wants and needs

From the interviews, I identified common themes by categorizing the feedback into wants, needs, pains, and opportunities.

I then created personas to represent different user experiences, which helped me maintain empathy throughout the design process and anticipate medication users’ needs.

"I want to know if my medication is impacting my mood day-to-day. Is it helping?"

"I feel isolated in my environment. I want to connect with people who face mental health challenges similar to my own."

"I need to undestand my diagnosis better. Are there alternatives to medication that could help me?"

Mapping out problems and solutions

To broaden the scope from which I could brainstorm design themes, I created an exhaustive list of user-relevant problems, ideas, quotes, emotions, thoughts, and potential solutions.

Analyzing competitors

To understand the competitive landscape, I analyzed apps with goals similar to my own and found that most used a simple "mood scale" ranging from "very good" to "very bad.

This binary system seemed too limiting, as it implied that high moods are always positive and low moods are always negative.

To encourage a perspective that moods are dynamic and all valuable for different reasons, I decided to include a second dimension in my mood scale to measure mood intensity.

Sketching the prototype

One of the most important takeaways from my research was the need to approach users holistically, not just as patients.

To acknowledge medication users within and beyond their diagnoses, I designed features that promoted a whole-person approach, such as an online forum for mental health discussions and a knowledge base for psychoeducation, in addition to tracking medication effects.

Digitizing the design

I converted my sketches into a digital prototype, integrating features that addressed key pain points and user insights from my research.

THE FINAL PRODUCT

An app that allows users to dynamically measure medication use and mood...



...connect anonymously with a safe community...



...and access educational content on mental health.
