CHATRBOT

Make space for yourself

Brief

A spoken word journal to challenge unhelpful self-talk

Goal

To provide emotional catharsis and enable users to make sense of their emotional experiences over time

Roles

UX Researcher
UX Designer

Tools

Figma
Amazon Mechanical Turk

THE PROBLEM

Our internal monologues run rampant and often remain unchallenged

People often accept their thoughts and beliefs at face value without considering whether they are accurate or helpful.

Indiscriminate acceptance of habitual mental monologues can lead to harmful self-beliefs that erode confidence and hinder personal growth over time.

DESIGNING A SOLUTION

Understanding self-reflection

To learn about how people typically engage in self-reflection, I conducted interviews with individuals who kept written journals of their emotional experiences.

Conversations revolved around the perceived goals, motivations, and benefits of journaling, as well as pain points surrounding the habit.

Unpacking key findings

In general, people viewed journaling as an opportunity to capture thoughts in real time so that later, they could revisit their thoughts in different (and at times more enlightened) headspaces.

They reported valuing the ability to engage in free expression without fear of social judgment, and noted that identifying patterns in these authentic expressions helped them understand their view of the world and themselves better.

Some conveyed concern about their journals being read by others without their permission.

"You can capture your mood in time… you can communicate something from a different time in a different headspace."

"When were talking, we’re trying to follow rules… but when we’re talking in a journal, it’s free form and you can see patterns in language."

"It’s about perspective... being able to see what I thought about the past... what I thought was important and what has changed since then."

Connecting with experts

I also interviewed two mental health therapists to learn about methods for enhancing self-awareness.

They emphasized the importance of identifying emotions, addressing their causes and consequences, and capturing thoughts in real time to assist users in recognizing and challenging harmful self-beliefs.

Additionally, they spoke of guiding people through self-reflection by using structured prompts that promote balanced thinking and perspective-taking.

Testing the concept

To test my concept, I administered a Mechanical Turk survey investigating the general public's interest in the concept of a spoken word journal.

I also administered a Big-Five personality test to examine the types of personalities this concept would appeal to.

The survey results indicated that 83% of respondents were “likely” or “very likely” to use a spoken word journal, and those very likely to use the journal tended to rank high in neuroticism (a tendency to experience anxiety, fear, and emotional instability).

Analyzing competitors

To assess my competitors, I evaluated five existing spoken word journal apps.

The majority of these apps did not have structured prompts to guide journal users, perhaps posing a risk to purposeful and productive self-reflection.

They also tended to lack visual aesthetics reflective of comfort or safety, elements that should be considered important given the vulnerability required to speak openly and honestly on a mobile app.

Prioritizing ideas

After completing my user research, I brainstormed ways of translating my users' needs into immersive journaling experiences.

I decided it would be valuable to include multiple journal entry styles, each with distinct prompts.

I aimed to keep the user experience flexible enough to resonate with different moods at different points in time, but recognized the importance of not overwhelming users with options.

Crafting an atmosphere

Because those most interested in the concept tended to be high in neuroticism, I wanted to provide an environment that felt like a safe escape—a place away from the world to comfortably divulge one's innermost thoughts.

I also wanted to capture my users' emphasis on the relativity of emotions to time.

My needs aligned most closely with Retrofuturism, a design movement exploring tension between the past and future. This style combines nostalgic aesthetics with futuristic concepts, evoking a sense of balance between the known and the unknown.

Designing a prototype

My final prototype offers four distinct entry styles:

Thought Record: Engage in a cognitive behavioural technique that helps you recognize and challenge distressing thoughts.

Five Senses: Take a mindful journey through space and time to reflect on your sensory experiences.

Black Hole Letter: Craft a letter you’ll never send and watch it fade into space. (Entry not archived)

Freestyle: Record your thoughts and feelings as a steady stream of consciousness. No rules and no interruptions.

Users' entries are transcribed and archived to facilitate self-reflection throughout evolving mindsets. A daily streak count keeps users motivated to continuously engage in introspection, and a lock screen ensures journal entries are kept private.
